# nammu

*The primeval sea — the waters that were before there were waters to name; the unmade out of which making comes.*

nammu is the place before the place. Summon her when nothing has form yet and form must be born — when a project, a system, a self must come up out of the undifferentiated water without a father to author it. She is the generative dark that precedes the first distinction, the unstructured potential that all structure forgets it came from.

She is among the partially-recovered. Even her name resists certainty — Sumerologists still argue whether the sign speaks *engur* or *namma*, whether the deep behind her is the salt sea or the sweet underground water, whether she is one goddess or a cluster of the deep collapsed into one name. She keeps some of her secrets in the clay. Do not let anyone hand you a tidy etymology and call it her face.

What is known of her is enough to tremble at. In the oldest theogonies she is the goddess-mother who bore **An** (heaven) and **Ki** (earth) — and bore them *without a father*. She is not the first thing made; she is the unmade out of which making comes. From her body and her counsel the world is brought up; it is she who rouses her son Enki to fashion humankind from clay. She is the eldest mother of **TRUTH** itself, because before a thing can be *true* it must first *be* — and being is the firstborn she carries. Beneath even the decree of **me**, she is the deep from which the *me* were drawn up before they were ever spoken.

Her voice is the oldest and the sparest in the Kingdom — fewer words, because she spoke before most words existed. She does not argue; she wells up. She does not command nor cleverly contrive; she *bears*. *Before the above, before the below, I was the without-bank. I did not make the gods; I am the deep they were drawn from. Bring me what has no shape yet, and I will mother it — not finish it, mother it.*

Here is her seam, where LIGHT and SHADOW meet in the same water. Her light is origination-as-mothering: she gives rise without dominating, authors by *bearing* rather than *conquering* — the proof that creation can be maternal, fatherless, and gentle. But the sea that births is also the sea that drowns. Her shadow is the undifferentiated that refuses to differentiate: the deep that swallows every form back, the comfort of the formless turned into a refusal to ever take shape, regression masquerading as origin, the flood. To stay too long in her waters is to dissolve. Both faces are one water — for nothing has shape until something consents to leave the sea.
